South Africa Economic Indicators Analysis (2015–2024)

Overview

An exploratory data analysis (EDA) of four key South African economic indicators across 40 quarters from 2015 to 2024. The analysis examines how unemployment, inflation, GDP growth, and the repo rate moved over a decade marked by structural economic challenges, a global pandemic, and shifting monetary policy.
Data is sourced from Stats SA and the South African Reserve Bank (SARB).

Business Context

South Africa's economy does not behave by the textbook. Unemployment has remained structurally high regardless of growth cycles, inflation is driven more by supply-side shocks than demand, and monetary policy operates under significant constraints. This analysis explores those dynamics through data.

Key Findings

1. Unemployment

Never dropped below 23% across the entire period
A misleading dip in Q2 2020 reflects the narrow definition of unemployment — discouraged work-seekers were excluded during hard lockdown
Peaked at 35.3% in Q4 2021 and has remained above 30% since

2. Inflation

Largely within the SARB's 3–6% target band from 2015 to 2020
Breached the upper target in 2022 driven by post-COVID supply chain disruptions, fuel prices, and food inflation
Returned within target by late 2024, creating room for rate cuts

3. GDP Growth

Weak and inconsistent throughout the period
Two technical recessions: 2018 and the historic COVID contraction of -16.4% in Q2 2020 — the worst quarterly decline in post-apartheid history
Post-2020 recovery has been slow, reflecting load shedding, logistics constraints, and low investment

4. Repo Rate

Cut aggressively during COVID to a historic low of 3.50%
Hiked sharply from 2021–2023 to combat inflation, reaching 8.25% by mid-2023
First cuts began in late 2024 as inflation stabilised within the target band

5. Correlation Analysis

No two indicators showed a strong correlation in isolation (all below 0.4)
The strongest relationship was between repo rate and inflation (0.33) — consistent with the SARB's mandate to use interest rates to manage price stability
Unemployment showed little relationship with GDP or inflation, reflecting the structural rather than cyclical nature of joblessness in SA

Key Takeaway: The weak correlations across all indicators tell their own story — South Africa's economic challenges are structural, not cyclical, and cannot be addressed through monetary policy alone.
